

### **1. Drawing and Describing Electrochemical Cells**

- **Half-cells:**
    - Must state: Metal electrode in 1 mol dm⁻³ solution of its ions (e.g., Ag(s) in Ag⁺(aq))[^1][^2].
    - For ions only (e.g., Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺), use a platinum (Pt) electrode in 1 mol dm⁻³ solution[^1][^2].
- **Circuit:**
    - Must show: Complete circuit with voltmeter, wires, and a labelled salt bridge linking the two half-cells[^1][^2].
    - Salt bridge must be labelled and shown touching both solutions[^1].
- **Standard conditions:**
    - State: 298 K (25°C), 100 kPa (or 1 atm), and 1 mol dm⁻³ concentrations[^1][^2].
    - If omitted, allow if shown for just one solution in diagram[^1].
- **Markscheme tip:** Do not allow “1 mole” for concentration; must be “1 mol dm⁻³” or “1 M”[^1].

### **2. Calculating Ecell and Using E° Values**

- **Formula:**
\$ E_{cell} = E^\circ_{right} - E^\circ_{left} \$[^1].
- **Order:**
    - Place the half-cell with the more positive E° value on the right[^1].
    - Electrons flow from the more negative to the more positive electrode[^1].
- **Markscheme tip:** Ignore sign for Ecell unless specifically asked[^1].
- **Comparison:**
    - The more positive/less negative system undergoes reduction; the more negative/less positive system undergoes oxidation[^1].

---

### **3. Writing Half-Cell and Overall Redox Equations**

- **Half-cell equations:**
    - Must be balanced for atoms and charge[^1].
    - Use electrons to balance charge.
- **Combining equations:**
    - Cancel electrons to form the overall equation[^1].
- **Markscheme tip:** Ignore state symbols, even if wrong[^1].

---

### **4. Identifying Reducing and Oxidising Agents**

- **Strongest reducing agent:**
    - The species with the most negative E° value[^1].
- **Strongest oxidising agent:**
    - The species with the most positive E° value[^1].
- **Markscheme tip:** If asked, state the species, not the ion/electrode name[^1].

---

### **5. Effect of Concentration and Conditions**

- **Changing concentration:**
    - Increasing concentration of oxidised species makes E° more positive[^1][^2].
    - Increasing concentration of reduced species makes E° more negative[^1][^2].
- **Le Chatelier’s principle:**
    - Used to explain shifts in equilibrium and changes in E°[^1].

---

### **6. Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)**

- **Description:**
    - Pt electrode, H₂ gas at 100 kPa, 1 mol dm⁻³ H⁺ solution, 298 K[^1].
- **Markscheme tip:** Used as reference (E° = 0.00 V)[^1].

---

### **7. Exam Technique and Common Pitfalls**

- **Always:**
    - Label salt bridge and voltmeter in diagrams[^1][^2].
    - Use Pt electrode for ions only[^1].
    - State all standard conditions[^1][^2].
- **Do not:**
    - Use “1 mole” for concentration or omit units[^1].
    - Omit the salt bridge or voltmeter in diagrams[^1][^2].
- **Examiners’ report:**
    - Candidates often lose marks by not stating standard conditions or not including all required components in diagrams[^1][^2].
    - Ensure equations are fully balanced and all species are included[^1].
